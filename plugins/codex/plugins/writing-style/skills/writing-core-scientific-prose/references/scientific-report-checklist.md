# Scientific Report Checklist

Use this checklist for English reports, progress PDFs, manuscripts, figure captions, and research slide text.

## Evidence Integrity

- Keep all numbers, units, denominators, baselines, confidence intervals, p-values, sample sizes, dates, versions, and labels unchanged unless the user asks for a correction.
- Do not convert a failed or inconclusive experiment into a success narrative.
- Preserve negative results, failed runs, missing controls, and uncertainty.
- State the comparison target. A sentence like "performance improved" is incomplete without "relative to what".
- Separate observation from interpretation.

## Scientific Claim Strength

- Direct result: "We observed..."
- Pattern across related results: "This pattern suggests..."
- Mechanistic explanation not yet tested: "One possible explanation is..."
- Planned work: "The next test is..."

Avoid:

- "proves" for observational or single-run evidence.
- "validates" without an explicit validation criterion.
- "robust" without stress tests, repeats, or external conditions.
- "generalizable" without out-of-distribution or external evidence.
- "significant" without defining statistical or practical significance.

## Reader Flow

Readers expect context before emphasis. Put familiar information first and the main new point last.

Weak:

> A 4-point Dice gain was observed after adding boundary loss, which addresses the small-organ segmentation problem.

Better:

> For small-organ segmentation, adding boundary loss increased Dice by 4 points, but the gain was concentrated in easy cases.

## Progress Report Pattern

For progress reports, use this prose pattern:

1. The question being answered.
2. The current change or experiment.
3. The key evidence.
4. The interpretation and competing explanation.
5. The risk or remaining uncertainty.
6. The next decision.

## Caption Pattern

A strong scientific caption is short but self-contained:

- Sentence 1: comparison, condition, and metric.
- Sentence 2: main observation or result.
- Sentence 3: panel definitions, abbreviations, error bars, or sample sizes if needed.

Do not use captions for long methods, generic summaries, or claims not visible in the figure.

## AI-Like Prose To Remove

Replace these patterns with evidence-specific wording:

- "It is important to note that..."
- "This highlights/underscores the importance of..."
- "In today's rapidly evolving landscape..."
- "This work lays the foundation for..."
- "Future work should continue to optimize..."
- Vague authority: "studies show", "experts believe", "the literature suggests" without a cited source.
- Symmetric filler: "accuracy, efficiency, and robustness" when only one dimension was measured.
- Over-broad endings: "These findings have important implications..." without naming the implication.

## Final Checks

- Can each paragraph be tied to a figure, table, experiment, source, or decision?
- Are uncertainties still visible?
- Is the strongest claim no stronger than the strongest evidence?
- Are captions readable without hunting through the body text?
- Does the text sound like a researcher making a bounded judgment?
